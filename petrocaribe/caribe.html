Petrocaribe is an oil alliance between Venezuela and 17 Caribbean countries that seeks to provide favorable trade deals for refined and unrefined petroleum. It formed in 2005, one year after many of the same countries created the Bolivarian Alliance for the Peoples of Our America, an economic alliance known as ALBA. It was created with the help of Cuba and was initiated by former Venezuelan president Hugo Chavez to foster an integrated economic system. ALBA also has its own digital currency, known as the Sucre, which was only used for the first time in 2010.

Petrocaribe does not offer a discount to member countries. Instead, it promises an extended timetable for payment, allowing countries to defer repayment for long periods of time, even decades. This structure has had a downside, though, allowing countries to rack up debt. In 2015, Jamaica had to sell $2 billion in bonds to pay down its debt to Venezuela. Introduction

At the time that it was started, Petrocaribe was dubbed by local media the best available option for the countries involved because the savings on oil would afford them more money to spend on social programs. The oil alliance was also meant to create energy independence for the countries involved. An analysis of shipping data, original documents and WikiLeaks, shows that Petrocaribe has a spotty record of delivering on its promises, much less even reporting its annual imports and exports.

For example, Saint Lucia only reported imports on oil in 2003 and 2008; meanwhile, Venezuela reported exports on oil to Saint Lucia in 2000, 2001 and 2004-2006, according to UN shipping data through Comtrade.

Petrocaribe is really more of a diplomatic measure, according to Luz Mely Reyes, a journalist and co-founder and CEO of Efecto Cocuyo, a Venezuelan news outlet. She said the strategy is to get support from countries because Venezuela knows the importance of having allies abroad, and they took advantage of the anti-imperialist movement and the polarization against the United States to create it.

Broadly speaking, the program has been plagued by complications, including badly managed supply chains and shoddy implementation. At best, Petrocaribe is a poorly run and dysfunctional organization. At worst, Venezuela uses Petrocaribe to buy political support for its human rights practices and suppression of free speech.
